Robotic Assistant for Finding Misplaced Objects

Course: BMED 8813 - Robotics Caregivers, Gatech, USA

Team members: Oluwatofunmi M Sodimu, Juan Antonio Robledo, Erin Kelly, Thanapol Tantagunninat, Kanishk., Daniel Lewis, and Nikhil Chittaluru

Robotics Caregiver Course Advisor: Dr. Charlie Kemp

Mild Cognitive Impairment (MCI) is an early-stage decline in memory, language perception, visual perception, and/or other cognitive abilities that is greater than expected for an individual’s age and education level, but that does not greatly impact their ability to perform everyday activities [1]. Research has shown that this condition is present in 3-19% of people over the age of 65 [1]. Individuals who experience a decline predominantly in their memory capacity are said to have Amnestic MCI. One common symptom of this condition is misplacing objects due to the inability to recall where the item was last kept [2],[3],[4]. This could cause patients to feel distressed, anxious, or even accuse their care partner of moving the object. To address this issue, we proposed the use of a robot to keep track of commonly misplaced objects and recall their location upon request.

In our implementation, the robot routinely navigates the home based on a pre-generated map and captures images of the surfaces found at each of the pre-set waypoints. These images are passed through an object classification model to identify the items within them. The names of the identified objects, timestamp, and location are stored in a JSON database. Upon request by the user, the database is scanned for all instances of the missing object, and the associated images are displayed on a screen for the user to confirm that it is indeed the object they are searching for. Finally, the robot leads the user to the object’s location and points at it.

Conclusion and Future Work

This project was presented at a Thinktank session with MCI patients in attendance. From this session, we were able to deduce multiple limitations of our implementation and able to garner information on certain features that could be implemented to improve the robustness of our system.

Some of these limitations include:

  • Misclassified objects due to poor lighting or proximity to camera
  • Unreliability of the system for finding occluded objects or objects within a drawer
  • Objects being overlooked if misplaced at some time between the robot’s routine scans

To address these limitations, we intend to explore the following methods:

  • Use of stretch’s hand-mounted camera to enable the robot to get close-up images of surfaces at different heights for more accurate object detection/classification.
  • Possible use of Ultra High Definition Radio Frequency Identification (UHR RFID) to find partially/fully occluded objects.
  • Scanning based on how frequently a user enters the room: In situations where the object is not immediately occluded, the robot may be able to spot the object if its scanning routine is optimized to coincide with certain motion patterns observed in the user [7]. The data used for optimization could be generated from a user-completed survey on how frequently they are likely to visit a particular room. The data could be further supplemented by tracking how frequently the missing object is found at a specific waypoint or in a specific room.
